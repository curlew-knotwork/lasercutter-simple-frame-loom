# 🧶 Laser-Cut Simple Frame Loom
Welcome! This project provides everything you need to laser-cut and build your own wooden frame loom, a matching storage box, and essential weaving tools.

The design uses finger-press fits, meaning the pieces are designed to snap together securely using just the pressure of your hands.

🛠 What You Will Need
Materials
6mm Birch Plywood: For the Loom Frame and the Stand.

3mm Birch Plywood: For the Storage Box, Shuttles, and Beater.

Sandpaper: Fine-grit (220 or higher) is essential for smoothing the wood.

Tools
Laser Cutter: Tested on the Epilog Fusion M2 (standard at Oodi Helsinki).

Optional: Wood glue (if your joints feel a bit loose) and a damp cloth.

## ⚠️ Important: "Clean Your Wood"
Laser cutting leaves char (carbon) on the edges of the wood.

The Problem: If left uncleaned, this black soot will rub off onto your yarn and ruin your weaving.

The Fix: Before assembly, lightly sand all edges or wipe them with a damp cloth. Focus especially on the "teeth" of the loom and the slots of the heddle bar where the yarn will rest.

## I. Setting Up
The Stand: Place the Loom Frame into the Stand cradle. It should tilt back slightly toward you for comfort.

The Beater: Pick up the handheld comb (the Beater). Ensure its teeth slide easily between the vertical threads of the loom.

## II. Warping (Stringing the Loom)
"Warp" threads are the vertical yarns that stay on the loom under tension.

Start: Tie your yarn to the bottom-left peg of the loom.

The Path: Pull the yarn up to the top notch directly above it, loop over the tooth, and bring it back down to the next bottom notch.

Tension: Keep the yarn taut like a guitar string.

Warning: If you see the wooden teeth of the frame bending (deflecting), your tension is too tight. Loosen it slightly to avoid snapping the wood.

Finish: Tie off the yarn at the last peg once the width is filled.

## III. Using the Heddle Bar
The Heddle Bar is the notched bar that keeps your threads organized and spaced evenly.

Threading: Make sure every single yarn strand sits in its own dedicated slot.

Creating the "Shed": To weave, you need to create a gap (a "shed") to pass your yarn through. Since this is a simple loom, use your shuttle or a flat stick to go over-under-over the threads.

The Trick: Turn the shuttle on its side to lift the threads up, creating a tunnel for your yarn to slide through easily.

IV. Using the Beater (The Comb)
The Beater is your most important tool for making actual fabric instead of a loose net.

The Action: After you pass your yarn horizontally, insert the Beater's teeth into the warp and "brush" the yarn down firmly toward you.

The "Rainbow" Tip: To keep your edges from pulling inward (making an hourglass shape), lay your yarn in a loose arch or "rainbow" before you beat it down. This gives the yarn enough slack to wrap around the edges comfortably.

## V. Your First Weave
Load the Shuttle: Wrap your "weft" (horizontal) yarn around the shuttle tool.

Over-Under: Pass the shuttle through the warp threads.

Beat: Push the yarn down.

Repeat: Go back the other way (reversing the over-under pattern).
